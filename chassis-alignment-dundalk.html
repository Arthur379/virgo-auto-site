Why it matters

Why chassis alignment matters

Every modern car is designed around its chassis geometry. The positions of the suspension mounting points, the angles of the subframe, the spacing of the engine mounts — all of it is set to millimetre precision at the factory. When that geometry shifts, even by a small amount, everything downstream is affected.

After a significant frontal or rear impact, the chassis rails can bend by just a few millimetres. You might not see it. The panels might look fine. The car might even drive. But the damage is there, and it shows up in ways that get worse over time: uneven tyre wear on one side, steering that pulls left or right, doors that no longer sit flush, a boot lid that catches on the seal.

More seriously, a bent chassis compromises the car's crash structure. Modern vehicles are engineered to absorb impact energy in a controlled sequence — crumple zones fold in a specific order to protect the occupants. If the chassis is already deformed from a previous impact, that sequence is disrupted. The next collision may not be absorbed the way the engineers intended.

A car that has been "repaired" cosmetically — panels straightened, bumper replaced, fresh paint — but has an underlying chassis misalignment is not a properly repaired car. It looks right. It is not right. The only way to know for certain is to measure the chassis against factory data, on a proper jig, at the correct reference points. That is what we do.

The equipment

How the chassis alignment jig works

Our chassis alignment process uses a bench-mounted jig — a heavy steel platform with a precision measuring system built in. This is not guesswork, and it is not eyeballing panel gaps. It is a controlled, repeatable engineering process.

Here is how it works, step by step:

  1. Clamping. The car is driven or rolled onto the bench and clamped at its factory-designated anchor points. These are specific locations on the underside of the car that the manufacturer uses during assembly. Clamping at these points means we are referencing the same datum the factory used.
  2. Initial measurement. A measuring system is attached to the car's chassis at multiple reference points — typically 12 to 20 locations depending on the vehicle. Each point is compared against the manufacturer's factory data. We record every reading. This gives us a precise map of what has moved, by how much, and in which direction.
  3. Hydraulic correction. Using hydraulic rams attached to the bench, we apply controlled force to the deformed sections of the chassis. Each pull is measured. We do not pull and hope — we pull, measure, adjust, and pull again. The process is gradual and precise.
  4. Verification. After each correction, measurements are taken again at all reference points. The goal is to bring every point back within the manufacturer's tolerance — typically ±2mm of the original factory specification.
  5. Documentation. Once the chassis is within tolerance at every reference point, we produce a final measurement report. This report shows the before readings, the after readings, and the factory specification for each point. You keep this document.

The result is a chassis that is geometrically correct — not approximately correct, not "close enough", but measured and verified against the same data the car was built to.

Scope of work

What we can straighten

01

Front chassis legs

The most common chassis damage we see. Frontal impacts push the chassis rails back and inward. This affects engine mounting, radiator support and front suspension geometry. We straighten both rails back to factory position and verify with jig measurements before release.

02

Rear chassis legs

Rear-end collisions push the back rails forward and often twist them laterally. This affects boot alignment, rear suspension geometry and fuel tank mounting. Rear chassis damage is frequently missed by shops that only assess the visible panels.

03

Sills and rocker structure

Side impacts deform the sill structure — the reinforced box sections that run along the bottom of the car between the front and rear wheels. These are critical load-bearing members. We straighten or section-replace sills and verify the cross-car dimensions on the jig.

04

Floor pan and subframe

Severe impacts can push the floor pan upward or twist the subframe out of position. This type of damage affects every system mounted to the floor — seats, seatbelt anchors, fuel lines, brake lines. We measure the floor against factory data and correct it hydraulically on the bench.

A different perspective

What a write-off shop sees vs what we see

When an insurance company writes off a car, they are making a financial calculation. The cost of repair exceeds a percentage of the car's market value, so they declare it uneconomical. That is a business decision. It is not an engineering assessment of whether the car can be made safe.

Many cars that are written off have chassis damage that is entirely correctable. The rails are bent, not cracked. The floor is pushed, not torn. The subframe is twisted by 8mm, not sheared. These are cars that, on a proper jig, can be brought back within manufacturer tolerance and documented to prove it.

We regularly work on cars that other shops have refused or that insurers have written off. If the chassis can be measured, corrected and verified, the car is structurally sound. If it cannot — if the damage is beyond what controlled straightening can fix — we will tell you honestly. We do not take on work we cannot stand behind.

Common questions

Chassis alignment FAQ

How do I know if my chassis is bent?

Common signs include uneven tyre wear, the car pulling to one side, doors or boot that no longer close properly, and uneven panel gaps. After any significant impact — even if the bodywork looks fine — the chassis may have shifted by a few millimetres. The only way to know for certain is to put it on a measuring jig. We offer a diagnostic measurement for a fixed fee, and if you go ahead with the repair that fee is deducted from the final price.

Can you save a car that has been written off?

Often, yes. An insurance write-off is an economic decision, not a structural one. The insurer decides it costs more to repair than the car is worth — but that does not mean the car cannot be made safe. If we can bring the chassis back within manufacturer tolerance and verify it with documented measurements, the car is structurally sound. We will inspect it honestly and tell you straight whether it can or cannot be saved.

Do you give a certificate after chassis work?

We supply a full measurement report showing before and after readings at every factory reference point. This documents that the chassis has been brought back within manufacturer tolerance. The report is useful for your own records, for any future buyer of the car, and as supporting evidence if you need to re-register a previously written-off vehicle.

Is a straightened chassis as strong as the original?

When done properly on a bench-mounted jig and brought back within manufacturer tolerance, yes. The metal is returned to its original position using controlled hydraulic force — it is not heated, cut or weakened. The chassis retains its designed crumple-zone behaviour and structural integrity. We would never release a car we were not confident in.
