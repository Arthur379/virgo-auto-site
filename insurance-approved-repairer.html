Guide · Your rights

Your insurance can't force you to use their approved repairer.

If you have been in an accident and your insurer is steering you towards their network of approved repairers, you should know this: in Ireland, you have the right to choose any crash repairer you want. The insurer can recommend. They cannot mandate. This guide explains what the rules actually say, how to assert your choice, and what to watch out for along the way.

You can choose your own repairer. Here is why.

In Ireland, the Central Bank's Consumer Protection Code sets out how insurance companies must treat their customers. Standard motor insurance contracts allow you to choose your own repairer. The insurer may have a list of approved or preferred bodyshops, and they may encourage you to use one, but they cannot refuse your claim or reduce your payout simply because you chose a different shop.

This right exists because you are the policyholder. You pay the premium. You are entitled to have your vehicle repaired to a proper standard by a repairer you trust. The insurer's obligation is to cover the reasonable cost of that repair, not to dictate where the work is done.

Most people do not know this. When the claims handler says "we'll send it to our approved repairer," it sounds like an instruction. It is not. It is a recommendation, and you are free to decline it.

What "approved repairer" actually means

An approved repairer is a bodyshop that has a commercial arrangement with an insurance company. The insurer directs a volume of work to that shop, and in return the shop agrees to certain conditions — typically discounted labour rates, the use of specific parts suppliers, and faster turnaround times that suit the insurer's claims cycle.

This arrangement is a business deal between two parties. It is not a quality certification. It does not mean the approved repairer is better than a non-approved one. It does not mean they use superior materials, employ more skilled technicians, or produce a higher standard of finish. It means they offered the insurer a price and a workflow that the insurer found commercially attractive.

Some approved repairers do excellent work. Some do not. The same is true of non-approved shops. The "approved" label tells you about the relationship between the shop and the insurer, not about the quality of the repair your car will receive.

Why insurers push approved repairers

Insurance companies have legitimate commercial reasons for preferring their own network. Understanding these reasons helps you make an informed decision about whether to follow their recommendation or go your own way.

  • Volume discounts: Approved repairers typically offer the insurer a discounted labour rate — sometimes 20 to 30 per cent below market rate. This lowers the insurer's average claim cost. The saving goes to the insurer, not to you.
  • Simpler claims handling: Approved repairers are integrated into the insurer's systems. They submit estimates electronically, use agreed parts pricing, and follow the insurer's process. This reduces administration for the claims team.
  • Faster turnaround (for the insurer): Because the approved repairer and the insurer have pre-agreed rates and processes, there is less back-and-forth over the quote. The claim closes faster. This benefits the insurer's metrics, though it does not always mean your car is repaired faster or better.
  • Not necessarily cheapest or best quality for you: The discounted rate the approved shop gives the insurer may mean the shop needs to work faster, use cheaper parts, or cut corners to maintain margins. This is not always the case, but it is a financial pressure that exists in the approved repairer model. When you choose your own repairer, you remove that pressure. Your repairer works to satisfy you, not the insurer's purchasing department.

When you should use your own repairer

Using the insurer's approved network is convenient. For routine bumper scuffs and minor bodywork, it can work perfectly well. But there are situations where choosing your own repairer makes more sense.

  • Specialist vehicles: If you drive a classic car, a heritage vehicle, or a performance car, you want a repairer who understands that vehicle. Approved repairer networks are set up for volume — modern hatchbacks and saloons. They may not have the tooling, the experience, or the patience for a car that needs careful, specialist attention.
  • Trust and history: If you have a local bodyshop that has looked after your cars for years, that relationship has value. They know your standards. They know your car. An approved repairer 40 kilometres away, chosen by an algorithm, does not.
  • Split-repair to save money: Some customers save money by splitting the job — having the structural or panel work done by a specialist (like us) and the painting done separately. Approved repairers generally will not accommodate this because they want the full job. If you want to manage the repair yourself to control cost and quality, you need your own repairer.
  • The approved repairer's quote seems inflated: If you have seen the estimate from the approved shop and it looks high, you are within your rights to get a second opinion. Some approved repairers quote to replace parts that could be repaired, because replacement is faster and easier for their workflow. A skilled panel beater may be able to save those parts — and save you money in the process.

How to assert your right

The process is straightforward. You do not need a solicitor or a formal complaint. Here are the steps.

1

Tell your insurer you want to choose your own repairer

When you report the claim, or when the claims handler contacts you, say clearly: "I would like to use my own repairer." Use those words. Do not ask for permission — you are informing them of your decision. They may try to talk you out of it. That is fine. Listen to what they say, then confirm your choice.

2

Ask for written confirmation of the claim authorisation amount

Request that the insurer provides, in writing (email is fine), the amount they are authorising for the repair. This figure should be based on the damage, not on where the work is done. If they have not yet assessed the damage, ask them to send an assessor or to accept a quote from your chosen repairer. Get the number in writing before the work begins.

3

Bring your car to your chosen repairer

Once the claim is authorised, take your vehicle to the repairer you have chosen. Share the insurer's authorisation details with the repairer so they know the scope and value of the approved work. If the repairer finds additional damage during the repair, they should document it and you should notify the insurer before proceeding with the extra work.

4

Invoice goes through the insurer — or you pay and claim back

Depending on your insurer and the arrangement, the repairer may invoice the insurer directly, or you may need to pay the repairer and then submit the invoice to your insurer for reimbursement. Ask your insurer which process they follow for non-approved repairers. In either case, keep a copy of the itemised invoice. Insurers accept clear, detailed invoices that show parts, labour and materials separately.

What if my insurer pushes back?

Some claims handlers will try to discourage you from using a non-approved repairer. They may say the process will take longer, that the repair will not be guaranteed, or that you will have more paperwork. Some of this is true (more on that below). But none of it changes your right to choose.

If the claims handler is persistent, you can say something like: "I understand you recommend your approved network. I have read my policy and I am exercising my right to choose my own repairer. Please confirm the authorised repair amount in writing so I can proceed."

If the insurer refuses to authorise the repair, delays unreasonably, or tries to reduce the payout because you chose a non-approved shop, you have a formal route of complaint. Start with the insurer's own complaints process. If that does not resolve the issue, you can escalate to the Financial Services and Pensions Ombudsman (FSPO). The FSPO is an independent body that investigates complaints against financial service providers in Ireland, including insurance companies. Their service is free to consumers.

Downsides of going non-approved — honest balance

We would not be giving you the full picture if we only talked about the benefits. There are genuine trade-offs when you step outside the approved network. You should know about them before you decide.

  • Sometimes slower: Approved repairers have a direct line to the insurer's systems. Quotes get approved quickly because rates are pre-agreed. When you use your own repairer, the insurer may need to send an independent assessor to verify the quote, and that adds time. How much time depends on the insurer and the complexity of the damage, but expect at least a few extra days.
  • More paperwork for you: With an approved repairer, the shop handles most of the claims paperwork. When you choose your own, you may need to coordinate between the repairer and the insurer yourself — forwarding quotes, chasing authorisation, submitting invoices. It is not complicated, but it is effort.
  • You may need to chase the insurer: Some insurers process payments to approved repairers automatically. Payments to non-approved shops may go through a different, slower channel. You might need to follow up to make sure your repairer gets paid, or to get your own reimbursement if you paid upfront.
  • Courtesy car may not be covered: Many approved repairers provide a courtesy car as part of their agreement with the insurer. If you go non-approved, the insurer may not cover a courtesy car, or your chosen repairer may not offer one. Check your policy wording — some policies include courtesy car cover regardless of which repairer you use, while others tie it to the approved network.

None of these downsides are deal-breakers. They are inconveniences. For many people, the ability to choose a repairer they trust, who will do the job properly, is worth a few extra phone calls and a slightly longer timeline.

Frequently asked questions

Does this apply to fleet insurance?

Fleet policies vary more than personal motor policies. Some fleet contracts include a mandatory repairer clause that the fleet operator agreed to in exchange for a lower premium. Check the specific wording in the fleet agreement. If the clause says "recommended" or "preferred" rather than "mandatory", you still have a choice. If it says "must" or "shall", the fleet operator (not the driver) agreed to that condition and it may be binding. If you are the fleet manager, you can renegotiate this clause at renewal.

What if it is not my fault — the other driver's insurer is paying?

When the third-party insurer is covering your repair, you absolutely have the right to choose your own repairer. The at-fault insurer may try to direct you to their approved network to control costs, but you are not their policyholder and have no contractual obligation to them. Get your own quote, have the work done, and submit the invoice. If liability is accepted, they must cover reasonable repair costs.

Can the insurer reduce my payout if I use a non-approved repairer?

Your insurer is obliged to cover the reasonable cost of repair, regardless of which repairer you choose. They cannot reduce the payout purely because you chose a non-approved shop. However, if your chosen repairer's quote is significantly higher than what the insurer considers reasonable, the insurer may query specific line items. This is why an itemised invoice matters — it lets the insurer see exactly what they are paying for and why. A clear, detailed invoice is much harder to dispute than a vague lump-sum quote.

Will my insurance premium go up more if I use a non-approved repairer?

No. Your premium at renewal is based on the claim itself — the fact that an incident occurred and what it cost — not on which repairer did the work. Whether you use an approved shop or your own makes no difference to how the claim is recorded or how it affects your renewal quote. The claims history is what matters, not the repair location.
