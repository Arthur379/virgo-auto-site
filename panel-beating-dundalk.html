What panel beating actually means

Panel beating is one of the oldest trades in motor repair, and one of the least understood. The term gets used loosely. Walk into most bodyshops and "panel beating" means cutting out the damaged section and bolting or welding in a brand-new panel. That's panel replacement, not panel beating. They're different skills with different costs and different outcomes.

Panel beating â€” reshaping the original metal

True panel beating means working the existing metal back to its original shape. Using hammers, dollies, spoons and heat, a skilled panel beater can push, pull and shrink damaged steel until it sits flush with the surrounding bodywork. The panel keeps its factory corrosion protection, its original spot welds, and its structural bonding. For the car owner, it means a cheaper repair and a car that retains its original metalwork â€” something that matters at resale.

Panel replacement â€” when the metal is beyond saving

Sometimes the damage is too severe. If the metal has torn, corroded through, or stretched so far that heat-shrinking can't bring it back, the panel needs replacing. We cut out the damaged section, fit a new or reconditioned panel, and weld it in. We match the factory seam positions and apply corrosion protection to every join. Panel replacement costs more because you're paying for the part as well as the labour, but it's the right call when the original panel won't hold shape.

Filler work â€” what we don't do

Some shops skip the metalwork and go straight to filler. A thick layer of polyester filler over a dented panel looks fine after paint, but it hides the damage rather than repairing it. Over time, filler shrinks, cracks and absorbs moisture. We don't do that. If we use filler at all, it's a skim coat of under 3 mm over metal that's already been worked to shape. If a panel needs more filler than that, it needs replacing.

Damage we handle

We work on steel and aluminium body panels across all makes. These are the most common jobs that come through our workshop in Dundalk.

Door dents & creases

Car park dents, shopping trolley marks, and sharp creases from side impacts. Most door dents can be beaten out without replacement, saving you the cost of a new door skin.

Wing & quarter panel damage

Front wings are bolt-on and straightforward to replace, but they can often be reshaped for less. Rear quarter panels are welded to the body â€” repair is almost always preferable to cutting and re-welding.

Bonnet dents & creases

Bonnets take damage from hailstones, falling objects and low-speed bumps. The broad, flat surface responds well to hammer-and-dolly work, and a good panel beater can restore the shape without any filler.

Boot & tailgate damage

Rear-end shunts, reversing incidents and heavy loads dropped in the boot. We straighten boot floors, reshape tailgate skins and re-align hinges so everything opens and closes the way it should.

Roof dents

Supermarket height barriers, falling branches and hailstone damage. Roof panels are large and visible, but they sit over a flat surface that makes hammer access easier than most people expect.

Sill & rocker panel damage

Kerb strikes, jack damage and corrosion. Sills are structural â€” they carry load in a side impact â€” so getting the shape and strength right matters. We repair or replace sills to factory spec.

Tools and techniques

Panel beating hasn't changed as much as people think. The tools are the same ones that coachbuilders used sixty years ago â€” what's different is the steel. Modern car panels are thinner, higher-strength and formed in more complex curves. That demands more precision, not less. Here's what we use in the workshop.

  • Hammer and dolly: The core of panel beating. The dolly sits behind the panel as an anvil while the hammer reshapes the metal from the outside. Different dolly profiles match different curves â€” flat, egg, toe, heel.
  • Shrinking disc and torch: When metal stretches (as it does in any impact), it needs to be shrunk back. A shrinking disc on an angle grinder, combined with controlled heat from an oxy-acetylene torch, contracts the metal without thinning it.
  • Stud welder and slide hammer: For dents where we can't get behind the panel, we weld small studs to the low spot and pull them out with a slide hammer. The studs are ground off and leave no trace after finishing.
  • Filler â€” sparingly: After the metal is within 2â€“3 mm of its final shape, a thin skim of polyester filler bridges the last imperfections. We never exceed 3 mm. If we can finish to bare metal, we do.

Every panel leaves our workshop at pre-paint standard: metal-finished, rust-treated and ready for primer. Your sprayer picks it up from there.

Why we don't paint

We're metalworkers, not painters. Painting is a separate trade that requires a controlled spray booth, colour-matching technology and clear-coat expertise. Shops that try to do both tend to do neither well. We'd rather be excellent at one thing than average at two.

This approach has a practical benefit for you. Because we don't mark up paint, you can choose your own sprayer â€” or use the one you already trust. Trade customers send panels to us bare and get them back ready for their own booth. Retail customers can bring us the damaged panel and collect it when it's straight, then take it to whichever paint shop offers the best price.

Frequently asked questions

How can I tell if a panel can be beaten or needs replacing?

It depends on the extent of the damage and whether the metal has stretched or torn. If the panel is creased but intact, it can usually be beaten back to shape. If the metal is torn, heavily corroded, or stretched beyond what heat-shrinking can correct, replacement is the better option. Send us a photo on WhatsApp and we'll tell you which approach suits your damage.

Is panel beating cheaper than panel replacement?

In most cases, yes. Panel beating uses the existing metal, so there's no cost for a new panel and less welding time. A typical dent repair starts from around €150, while a full panel replacement starts from €800 plus the cost of the panel itself. Beating also preserves the original factory seams and corrosion protection, which can matter at resale.

Do you use filler?

We use filler sparingly â€” never more than 3 mm â€” and only after the metal has been worked as close to its original shape as possible. We don't use filler to hide poor metalwork. If a panel needs more than a skim coat, we'll recommend replacement instead. Our goal is a repair that's structurally sound, not just cosmetically smooth.

How long does a typical panel repair take?

A single dent or crease usually takes half a day to a full day. More complex damage involving multiple panels or partial replacement can take two to five days. We'll give you a clear timeframe when we assess the damage, and we'll let you know if anything changes during the repair.
