What drives the cost of crash repair

Five factors determine whether your repair bill lands at the low end or the high end of the ranges above. Understanding them helps you know what to expect before you get a formal quote.

1. Severity of damage

This is the obvious one. A shopping-trolley dent in a door skin is a fundamentally different job from a front-end collision that has pushed the chassis rails back. More damage means more labour hours, more consumables and potentially more parts. Severity also determines whether the repair is cosmetic (panel work only) or structural (chassis and reinforcement work). Structural repairs take longer, require specialist equipment like a chassis alignment jig, and cost significantly more.

2. Car make and model

Parts availability matters. A replacement wing for a 2018 Volkswagen Golf is cheap, widely stocked and arrives next day. A replacement quarter panel for a 2020 Alfa Romeo Giulia may need to be ordered from Italy with a three-week lead time and a price tag to match. Labour also varies â€” some cars are designed to be repaired easily, others are not. German and Japanese cars tend to be well-documented with good parts supply. Some French and Italian marques are harder to source for, which pushes up cost and turnaround.

3. Labour rates

Workshop labour rates in Ireland vary by location. A Dublin or Cork city-centre bodyshop might charge €60 to €80 per hour. A regional specialist in a town like Dundalk typically charges €40 to €55 per hour. The work quality can be identical â€” you are paying a location premium, not a skill premium. This difference adds up quickly on a job that takes 20 or 30 hours.

4. Paint area

Paint is often the single most expensive part of a crash repair. A full door respray costs €300 to €600. A bonnet is €400 to €700. If the damage spans multiple panels, the paint bill can exceed the metalwork bill. This is one reason our split-repair approach saves money â€” you pay a dedicated panel specialist for the metalwork and a dedicated sprayer for the paint, with no middleman marking up either job.

5. Whether structural work is needed

If the crash has bent the chassis, subframe or structural reinforcements, the repair enters a different category. Chassis alignment requires a bench-mounted jig and precise measurement to bring the car back within manufacturer tolerance. This is specialist work â€” not every bodyshop has the equipment or the training. Structural repairs add €400 to €4,000 or more to the total cost, depending on severity.

Insurance vs cash â€” which is actually cheaper?

Most people assume that claiming on insurance is the obvious choice after a crash. It often is not. Here is the honest maths that most bodyshops and brokers will not walk you through.

When you make an insurance claim for crash repair, three costs kick in that have nothing to do with the repair itself:

  • Excess: The standard excess on Irish motor policies is typically €250 to €750. This is the amount you pay out of pocket before the insurer covers anything. Some policies have higher voluntary excesses of €500 to €1,000 if you opted for a lower premium.
  • No-claims discount loss: A single claim typically drops your no-claims discount by one to two steps. In practice, this costs you €200 to €500 per year in higher premiums, and it takes 3 to 5 years to earn it back. Over that period, the total cost of lost NCD ranges from €600 to €2,500.
  • Premium hike: On top of losing your NCD, your base premium often increases by 15 to 30 per cent after a claim â€” even a non-fault claim. This loading can persist for 3 to 5 years before it drops off your record.

Add these together and the true cost of claiming becomes clear.

Worked example

Suppose your car needs €2,500 worth of crash repair. You have a €500 excess and a full no-claims discount. If you claim:

  • You pay €500 excess up front
  • Your NCD drops, costing you roughly €300 per year extra for 3 years = €900
  • Total real cost to you: €1,400

If you pay cash at a specialist who uses a split-repair model (separate panel shop and sprayer, no middleman markup), the same repair might cost €1,800 to €2,200. That is more than the €1,400 insurance route in this example â€” but only just. And you keep your full NCD, avoid the premium hike, and maintain a clean claims history for future quotes.

For repairs under €1,500, the cash route almost always wins. For repairs between €1,500 and €3,000, it depends on your excess and NCD value. For repairs above €3,000, insurance usually makes more financial sense â€” but it is still worth getting a cash quote to compare.

Where to save vs where not to cut corners

Not all crash repairs are equal. Some are good candidates for cost-saving measures. Others are not. Here is a straightforward guide to where you can afford to be thrifty and where you absolutely should not.

OK to save: minor cosmetic dents

If the damage is purely cosmetic â€” a door ding, a car-park scrape, a shallow dent with no structural involvement â€” skip the insurance claim and pay cash. The repair cost is likely under €500, which is close to or below your excess anyway. A specialist panel beater can often reshape minor dents for €150 to €300 without any paint work at all.

Do not skimp on: chassis and structural work

If the chassis or structural members are damaged, this is not the place to shop on price alone. Chassis alignment requires a jig, documented measurements and experience. A bad structural repair compromises the car's crash safety â€” the crumple zones, the roll cage integrity, the airbag mounting points. Go to a specialist with the right equipment and insist on documented jig measurements before and after the repair. The cheapest quote for structural work is often the most expensive mistake you can make.

OK to save: used panels from breakers for older cars

If you are repairing a car that is 8 or 10 years old, there is no reason to buy a brand-new OEM panel when a good-condition used panel from a breakers yard costs a third of the price. We source used panels regularly for older vehicles. As long as the panel is straight, corrosion-free and the correct part number, it welds in identically to a new one. This can save €200 to €800 on a single panel replacement.

Do not: accept filler-over-damage shortcuts

Some shops will skim a thick layer of filler over damaged metal and call it a repair. It looks fine after paint. But filler is not metal. It does not absorb impact. It shrinks over time, cracks in cold weather and absorbs moisture that causes rust underneath. If a shop is quoting significantly less than everyone else for panel work, ask why. The answer is usually filler. A proper repair means the metal is worked back to shape first, with filler used only as a thin finishing coat â€” never more than 3 mm.

Frequently asked questions

Why won't shops give prices over the phone?

Because crash damage varies enormously even between cars that look similar. A dented door might need 2 hours of work or 8 hours depending on whether the inner skin is damaged, whether the metal has stretched, and whether the hinges have shifted. Any shop that quotes without seeing the damage is guessing â€” and they will either overcharge to cover themselves or underquote and add extras later. A photo-based quote is the minimum responsible approach. We ask for 3 to 4 photos on WhatsApp and quote within 2 hours.

Do I have to use my insurer's approved repairer?

No. In Ireland you have the legal right to choose your own repairer. Your insurer may recommend or steer you towards their approved network, but you are not obliged to use them. You can get the work done wherever you choose and submit the invoice to your insurer. Some people find that paying cash for smaller repairs and skipping the claim entirely works out cheaper once you factor in excess, premium increases and no-claims discount loss.

What if the repair costs more than the car's worth?

If you are going through insurance, the insurer may write the car off â€” meaning they consider it uneconomical to repair relative to its market value. But "uneconomical" is their calculation, not yours. Many cars written off by insurers are structurally sound and perfectly repairable. If you own the car outright and the chassis can be brought within manufacturer tolerance, a cash repair can put a written-off car back on the road safely and legally. We will always tell you honestly whether a repair makes sense.

Can I get an independent quote during an insurance claim?

Yes. You are entitled to get your own independent assessment and quote at any point during the claims process. This can be useful if you believe the insurer's approved repairer is overcharging, underscoping the work, or if you want to compare a cash repair against claiming. We provide detailed, itemised quotes that you can use for comparison or submit to your insurer.
