The split-repair guide: two specialists, half the cost.

Most people assume crash repair has to happen under one roof. It does not. By splitting the job between a panel beater and a separate sprayer, you keep more money in your pocket — typically 20-40% less than a full-service body shop quote.

The status quo

The traditional full-service model

When your car gets damaged, the default path is straightforward. You bring it to a body shop, they do everything — strip the panels, pull or replace the metal, prime, paint, reassemble, polish — and hand you back a finished car. One invoice, one point of contact, job done.

That convenience has a cost. A full-service body shop carries significant overhead. They need a spray booth (often costing over €80,000 to install), EPA-compliant extraction, paint inventory, mixing equipment, and the qualified staff to operate all of it. They also need admin staff to deal with insurance companies, chase assessors, and manage claims paperwork. All of these costs are baked into every job that comes through the door.

On top of that, bundling everything under one roof means the shop sets one margin across the entire job. There is no transparency about what the metalwork costs versus what the paint costs versus what the overhead adds. You pay a single number and trust that it is fair. Sometimes it is. Often there is room to do better.

This model works well for shops and for insurance companies. It does not always work well for the person paying the bill — especially if you are paying out of pocket rather than claiming on your policy.

The alternative

The split model

The split-repair approach breaks the job into its two natural halves: metalwork and paint. Each half goes to a specialist who focuses on that one thing, without carrying the overhead of the other.

Specialist 1 — the panel beater (us)

We handle everything structural and metal. Panel straightening, dent repair, chassis pulling on our jig, welding, cutting, replacement panel fitting. We do not paint. We do not own a spray booth. We do not carry paint inventory. That means our premises costs are lower, our staffing is leaner, and our quotes reflect the actual cost of metalwork — nothing else. When the car leaves us, the metal is straight, the structure is sound, and the panels are ready for primer and paint.

Specialist 2 — the sprayer of your choice

A dedicated spray shop receives a car with all the metalwork already done. They do not need to pull dents or straighten frames — they prime, colour-match, spray, blend adjacent panels, and polish. Because they are not doing metalwork, they can focus entirely on the finish. Many sprayers prefer receiving pre-repaired panels because it simplifies their workflow and reduces their risk.

You — the customer

You coordinate two drop-offs instead of one. In return, you remove the bundling markup, you see exactly what each stage costs, and you keep control over who does the work. You choose your own sprayer based on quality and price, rather than accepting whoever the body shop employs in-house.

Timeline

The split approach typically adds 1 to 3 working days compared to a full-service job. Most of that is the transport between our workshop and the sprayer. For the savings involved, most of our customers consider that a reasonable trade-off.

Split repair

The saving comes from three places. First, we do not carry paint-booth overhead, so our metalwork rate is lower. Second, the sprayer receives a prepped car, so their job is quicker and simpler. Third, there is no single-shop markup bundling both trades together. Each specialist charges for what they actually do, and nobody is loading the other trade's costs into their price.

Right fit

When split repair makes sense

Split repair is not for every situation, but it is a strong option in these common scenarios.

  • Moderate panel damage (one or two panels). This is the sweet spot. The metalwork is substantial enough to justify a specialist, and the paint job is straightforward enough for any competent sprayer.
  • Chassis or structural work is needed. We have the jig and the expertise to pull chassis rails back to manufacturer spec. Most spray shops do not. If your car needs structural correction before it can be painted, the split approach makes particular sense.
  • You already know a good sprayer. If you have a sprayer you trust — or one your neighbour swears by — the split model lets you use them rather than whoever a body shop has in-house.
  • Cash repair (not going through insurance). When you are paying out of pocket, every euro matters. The split approach gives you direct control over costs and removes the insurance-admin overhead that body shops build into their pricing.
  • Older car where budget matters. A 2012 car with a bent wing does not need a premium full-service experience. It needs the metal straightened properly and a decent coat of paint. Split repair gets you both without overspending.
  • Trade work (garages splitting the job). Many garages and mechanics already split work this way. They send the metalwork to us, handle the spray themselves or send it to a partner sprayer, and manage the customer relationship. It is standard practice in the trade.
Honest assessment

When split repair does not make sense

We would rather tell you upfront than waste your time. There are situations where a full-service body shop is the better choice.

  • Very small jobs not worth moving the car twice. If the repair is a single small dent and a touch-up, the cost of transporting the car between two workshops eats into the saving. For minor cosmetic work, a one-stop shop is more practical.
  • Very urgent insurance jobs where turnaround beats savings. If your insurer is managing the claim and providing a courtesy car, the speed and convenience of a single approved repairer outweighs the potential saving. The insurance company is paying anyway, and they want one point of contact.
  • Very complex multi-panel jobs where coordination overhead adds up. If the car needs six panels repaired, structural work, and a full respray, the coordination between two workshops becomes more involved. The saving per panel shrinks, and the extra days add up. In these cases, a full-service shop with everything under one roof may be more efficient.
Practical advice

Finding a good sprayer

The split-repair model is only as good as the sprayer you choose. Here is what to look for and what to ask before you commit.

Do they have a proper spray booth? A dedicated downdraft booth produces a cleaner finish than an open workshop. Ask to see it. If they are spraying in a corner of a garage with plastic sheeting, keep looking.

How do they colour-match? Good sprayers use a spectrophotometer to read the exact colour of your car, then mix to match. Cheaper operations rely on the manufacturer's colour code alone, which can drift from the actual colour after years of sun exposure and weathering. Ask how they match.

Are they EPA-compliant? In Ireland, spray operations need to comply with environmental regulations around solvent emissions. A legitimate sprayer will have the correct extraction, filtration and waste handling in place. This is not just a legal question — it tells you whether they run a professional operation.

Will they blend adjacent panels? A good sprayer will blend the colour into the panels either side of the repair so there is no visible line where new paint meets old. Ask whether blending is included in their quote or charged extra.

If you do not have a sprayer in mind, we can recommend three spray shops in Louth and Meath that we trust. We work alongside them regularly, our customers have been happy with their results, and we do not take a commission or referral fee. Just ask when you contact us.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

Is it really cheaper to use two separate specialists?

In most cases, yes. A full-service body shop bundles metalwork, painting, admin and overhead into one price. When you split the job, you remove the bundling markup and pay each specialist directly for what they actually do. On a typical two-panel repair, our customers save between 20% and 40% compared to a full-service quote. The saving is largest on moderate jobs where panel work and paint are roughly equal portions of the bill.

How do I coordinate between panel beater and sprayer?

It is simpler than you might think. We complete all metalwork and structural repairs first, then let you know when the car is ready to collect. You drive it to your chosen sprayer. There is no complicated handover — the sprayer can see exactly what has been done and what needs painting. If you want, we can speak to the sprayer directly to confirm scope. Most of our split-repair customers say the coordination takes one or two phone calls at most.

What if the sprayer finds more damage after your work?

It happens occasionally. If the sprayer discovers additional metalwork once panels are stripped and prepped for paint, we will take the car back and sort it at the original quoted rate — no surprises. This is rare because we inspect thoroughly before quoting, but we stand behind our work either way.

Do you recommend specific sprayers?

Yes. We work alongside several spray shops in Louth and Meath and can recommend three sprayers we trust for quality and turnaround. We do not take a commission or referral fee — we recommend them because their work is good and our customers have been happy with the results. You are also free to use any sprayer you prefer.
